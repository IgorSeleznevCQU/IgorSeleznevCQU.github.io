Outrun

Medium: Vector Graphics (Adobe Illustrator)


Recently I've been fascinated by the retro culture of the 1980s, and the retro trend has been getting very popular in pop-culture. It's quite obvious why: while some of it might simply be nostalgia, the synthesiser music of the era is very melodic, the films are cheesy with heavily romanticised characters, and the graphics are bold and memorable, often stylised with heavy use of neon lights and colours.

This piece is intended to be used as a wallpaper and is an exercise in vector linework and gradients to reproduce a recognisable art style.

Cityscape

Medium: Vector Graphics (Adobe Illustrator)


This piece is an exercise in using basic shapes, lines, and grayscale tones to create an impression of a looming cityscape without fully rendering the individual buildings.

Vector Apple

Medium: Vector Graphics (Adobe Illustrator)


This piece is an exercise in using Adobe Illustrator's grid tool to recreate an organic shape (in this case, an apple). While oftentimes vector graphics are used for fairly basic or stylised shapes and gradients, this exercise proves that it is quite possible to render more realistic objects as well.

This artwork was created as part of the Digital Media course at CQUniversity.

Dungeon Dash Concept Artwork

Medium: Raster Graphics (Adobe Photoshop)


These are some of the examples of concept artwork developed for a video game called Dungeon Dash. The game is a fairly basic dungeon crawler where the player dons a suit of knight armour and attempts to escape a dungeon, getting rich and potentially saving a princess in the process. The concept art was used for developing a paper prototype for the game and was partly inspired by artwork for the Munchkin board game.

This artwork was created as part of the Digital Media course at CQUniversity.

Pixel Art Joker

Medium: Raster Graphics (Paint.Net)


At one point I was very interested in pixel art. This was in part due to the prevalence of indie games that used this art style for sprites and tiles, but also because of its extremely minimalist approach to rendering objects. It is deeply fascinating that the human eye can interpret a handful of pixels as vehicles, plants, and creatures.

This is a pixel art rendition of Heath Ledger's Joker from The Dark Knight. Originally only 44 pixels long and wide, this work depicts the Joker sitting in the GCPD interrogation room with Batman standing behind him, ready to begin the "questioning".
